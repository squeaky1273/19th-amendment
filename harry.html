State Rep. Harry T. Burn (R) - TN

(November 12, 1895 - February 19, 1977)


Bio

Harry was a 24 yr old state representative in the Tennesse House of Representatives as a Republican. He was from Niota in McMinn County and represented it in the state House. He was the youngest memeber elected to the Tennesse State House at the age of 22. He had a widowed mother while in his early years in the House.

Impact


Background of Situation

Tennesse is the swing state that could decide whether or nor the amendment gets the much needed 3/4 majority of states. Other states had either decied to vote down the proposal or even not take up a vote for the amendment. Tennesse was in a 48-48 stalemate over a motion to delay a ratification vote thus the amendment stayed on the House floor.


The Letter that Changed the Country

Burn had voted to delay a vote on the amendment and thus a deadlock was created. That day, Burn had came in with a red rose on his lapel which many took as a sign that he would always vote no. He did for the first 2 roll call, until it came to the third one. Unbeknownst to everyone else in the room, Burn recieved a letter form his mother, urging him to vote for ratification. The letter read:

“Dear Son, … Hurray and vote for Suffrage and don’t keep them in doubt. I noticed Chandlers’ speech, it was very bitter. I’ve been waiting to see how you stood but have not seen anything yet…. Don’t forget to be a god boy and help Mrs. Catt with her “Rats.” Is she the one that put rat in ratification, Ha! No more from mama this time. With lots of love, Mama.” from mama this time. With lots of love, Mama.”

Febb Burn

He was in a bind because his re-election was coming up and his constituents were against ratification. The dilemma was between thinking about his re-election bid or following his mother's wishes. When the call came to Burn, he voted 'Aye' giving the majority vote in Tennesse to make Tennesse the 26th and final state to ratify the 19th amendment . This caused heads to turn, as many thought he actually meant to vote "Nay". This vote caused an uproar among anti-suffragists. Burn was accused of being a traitor to manhood and being bought off.

Later, Burn narrowly survived re-election and remained honored to be that deciding vote.

“I knew that a mother’s advice is always safest for a boy to follow and my mother wanted me to vote for ratification, - I appreciated the fact that an opportunity such as seldom comes to a mortal man to free 17 million women from political slavery was mine.”

Harry T. Burn
